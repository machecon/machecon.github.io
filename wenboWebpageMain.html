Research

  • Work in progress
    • Cumulative Innovations, Growth and Optimal Patent Protection (with Amy Sun)
  • Working papers
    • Skill-Replacing Technological Change and the Skill Premium: Theory and Evidence
      • I study the effects of product innovation and process innovation on the skill premium at the industry level. I find that industries which have proportionally more firms reporting product innovations than process innovations, also tend to exhibit a higher skill premium. To better understand this phenomenon, I develop a dynamic model in which firms conduct both types of innovation endogenously. In the model, product innovation introduces new technology, which tend to complement high-skilled labour. Process innovation simplifies existing technology, which tends to complement low-skilled labour. The model generates a bi-directional relationship between the skill premium and the development of the two types of innovation. I estimate these effects based on the model using the Community Innovation Survey and the Structure of Earnings Survey from the Eurostat database.
    • Hollowing Out and Slowing Growth: The Role of Process Innovations
      • I develop an endogenous growth model that can accommodate the hollowing out of the middle class and the slowing down in labour productivity growth simultaneously. The key element of the model is the process of technological maturity. When a piece of new technology enters the economy, it requires high-skilled workers in its implementation. Over time, the technology becomes more user-friendly so that lower skilled workers can also operate it. I assume firms conduct process innovation to achieve the next level of technological maturity, for cost-saving purposes. Process innovation increases the range of technologies that a lower skilled worker can operate, therefore it is of value to growth. Process innovation also reallocates labour demand for different skilled worker groups and thereby affects the income distribution. I calibrate the model to European data between 2002 and 2014 and infer indirectly the relative intensity of process innovation occurred at different production stages.
